EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Purpose of Assessment

This skills assessment exercise demonstrates competencies essential for an Institutional Research Specialist position, specifically:

  1. Development of rigorous survey instruments for university populations
  2. Application of psychometric validation and established research methodologies
  3. Design of analytical frameworks to generate actionable insights for university leadership
  4. Translation of complex data into strategic recommendations

Scope and Methodology

This assessment addresses the scenario wherein the Deans of the Colleges and Schools seek to assess faculty experience across the University to inform efforts to attract and retain top faculty. The assessment encompasses:

Topics of Interest: Overall satisfaction, workload, teaching support, professional development, work-life balance, hiring/retention/tenure/promotion perspectives, and sense of belonging

Methodological Approach: Evidence-based survey design drawing upon validated instruments from leading national surveys (COACHE, ModernThink, AAU Campus Climate Survey) combined with original contributions addressing contemporary faculty experience challenges

Analytical Framework: Multi-dimensional analysis strategy enabling comparative assessment across colleges, equity analysis across demographic groups, predictive modeling of retention risk, and segmentation by career stage and discipline

Key Deliverables

This report provides:

  1. A comprehensive 30-question survey instrument with complete annotations citing psychometric sources, theoretical reasoning, and anticipated insights for Deans
  2. Background research summary documenting the literature review and established best practices informing survey design
  3. Description of analytical opportunities the survey data enables, including comparative, equity, and predictive analyses
  4. List of 10 potential analyses with sample visualizations demonstrating how survey results translate into actionable intelligence
  5. Interactive web-based dashboard prototype demonstrating data visualization capabilities using simulated survey responses

The survey instrument balances comprehensiveness with brevity (10-12 minute completion time) while maintaining psychometric rigor. Strategic demographic stratification enables sophisticated equity analysis, particularly examining intersectional experiences of underrepresented faculty. The analytical framework prioritizes actionable insights enabling Deans to make evidence-based decisions regarding resource allocation, policy interventions, and strategic initiatives to enhance faculty experience and retention.

BACKGROUND RESEARCH

Description of Background Research Conducted

This survey instrument draws on established best practices in higher education faculty assessment, synthesizing validated items from leading national surveys. The primary research foundation includes the Collaborative on Academic Careers in Higher Education (COACHE) Faculty Job Satisfaction Survey, widely considered the gold standard for faculty climate assessment with psychometric validation across hundreds of institutions. COACHE's multi-dimensional framework covering workload, compensation, climate, tenure clarity, and resources provides the theoretical backbone.

Additional sources include ModernThink's "Great Colleges to Work For" survey, which emphasizes overall satisfaction and work-life balance measures that predict retention. The Association of American Universities (AAU) Campus Climate Survey informed belonging and inclusion items, particularly important given national attention to faculty diversity and equity. Psychological safety questions adapted Edmondson's organizational behavior research, recognizing that voice and safety drive innovation and honest institutional improvement.

Literature review revealed several critical factors predicting faculty satisfaction and retention: compensation competitiveness, workload manageability, tenure process clarity and fairness, work-life balance, sense of belonging, research support, teaching resources, and quality of departmental leadership. Studies consistently show that underrepresented faculty (women, faculty of color) experience lower satisfaction, greater service burden, and reduced belonging, making demographic intersectional analysis essential.

Original contributions include questions addressing contemporary concerns: administrative burden (frequently cited in Chronicle of Higher Education surveys), collaboration opportunities (increasingly important for interdisciplinary research), and specific teaching resource needs (moving beyond general satisfaction to actionable specifics). The survey balances brevity (10-12 minutes, ~30 questions) with comprehensiveness, following survey design principles that response rates decline significantly beyond 15 minutes. Strategic use of display logic (e.g., showing non-tenure track questions only to relevant respondents) reduces burden while maintaining depth.

ANALYTICAL OPPORTUNITIES

Description of Potential Analytical Opportunities

This survey enables sophisticated analysis addressing the Deans' strategic goals around faculty attraction and retention. The combination of satisfaction outcomes, demographic variables, and experience predictors supports multiple analytical approaches that generate actionable insights.

Comparative analysis across colleges/schools will identify which units have the strongest and weakest faculty experience, enabling targeted interventions and best practice sharing. For example, if one college demonstrates significantly higher satisfaction and retention, investigating their practices (leadership approach, workload distribution, resource allocation) could inform other units.

Regression and correlation analysis can identify which factors most strongly predict overall satisfaction and retention risk. This prioritizes where the institution should invest limited resources—if research funding emerges as the top predictor while classroom facilities show weak association, focus investment accordingly. Employee Net Promoter Score (eNPS from Q9) correlates with actual turnover and can benchmark against national norms.

Equity and inclusion analysis through demographic cross-tabulations reveals potential disparities. Do women report lower belonging than men? Do faculty of color experience less psychological safety? Does service burden fall disproportionately on underrepresented faculty? Such gaps identify DEI challenges requiring intervention. Intersectional analysis (e.g., women of color in STEM) may reveal compounded disadvantages.

Segmentation analysis by career stage, appointment type, and discipline reveals different needs. Junior tenure-track faculty may need mentorship while senior faculty need research support; lecturers may prioritize teaching resources while research faculty focus on funding. Discipline differences (STEM vs. humanities) may require tailored approaches.

Qualitative analysis of open-ended responses provides context and specificity. Text analysis can identify common themes, while representative quotes humanize quantitative findings for leadership presentations. Triangulating quantitative patterns with qualitative examples creates compelling narratives for action.

Longitudinal tracking (if repeated annually) monitors progress, assesses intervention effectiveness, and identifies emerging issues. Benchmarking against COACHE or peer institution data contextualizes Notre Dame's performance.

ANALYSIS 1: COLLEGE-LEVEL SATISFACTION BENCHMARKING

Method: Descriptive statistics with bar chart visualization comparing mean overall satisfaction scores (Q8) across colleges/schools

Insight for Deans: Identifies which colleges have the strongest and weakest faculty satisfaction, enabling targeted interventions and resource allocation. Colleges significantly below the university mean require immediate attention.

Visualization: Horizontal bar chart showing satisfaction scores (1-5 scale) by college with university mean reference line

Actionable Recommendations:

  • Colleges scoring below 3.0: Conduct follow-up focus groups to identify specific drivers of dissatisfaction
  • High-performing colleges: Document and share best practices (leadership approach, resource allocation, culture-building strategies)
  • Enable peer learning between high and low-performing units

Dashboard Location: Chart 1 - "Overall Satisfaction by College"

ANALYSIS 2: EMPLOYEE NET PROMOTER SCORE (eNPS) BY COLLEGE

Method: Calculate eNPS from Q9 using formula: (% Extremely Likely - % Extremely/Somewhat Unlikely) × 100. Scores range from -100 to +100.

Insight for Deans: eNPS predicts faculty retention and recruitment success. Negative scores indicate colleges struggling to attract talent and at high risk for faculty departure. Benchmark against national norms (higher education eNPS typically ranges from +10 to +30).

Visualization: Horizontal bar chart with color coding (negative = red, positive = green) showing eNPS by college

Actionable Recommendations:

  • Negative eNPS colleges: Address reputation damage through targeted retention initiatives, leadership development, and culture interventions
  • Investigate why faculty wouldn't recommend the institution; correlate with other survey items to identify drivers
  • Use positive eNPS colleges as recruitment marketing examples

Dashboard Location: Chart 2 - "Employee Net Promotor Score (eNPS)"

ANALYSIS 3: FACULTY EXPERIENCE EQUITY ANALYSIS BY GENDER

Method: Cross-tabulation of key experience metrics (satisfaction, belonging, psychological safety) by gender identity (Q6), displayed as grouped bar chart

Insight for Deans: Reveals gender equity gaps in faculty experience. Research consistently shows women faculty report lower satisfaction, belonging, and psychological safety. Identifying gaps is the first step toward targeted DEI interventions.

Visualization: Grouped bar chart comparing men, women, and non-binary faculty across multiple metrics

Actionable Recommendations:

  • Significant gaps (>0.5 points on 5-point scale): Require immediate DEI action such as bias training, mentoring programs, or policy review
  • Examine service burden distribution (Q15) by gender to identify inequitable committee assignments
  • Create faculty affinity groups and support networks for underrepresented genders

Dashboard Location: Chart 3 - "Faculty Experience Equity Analysis"

ANALYSIS 4: RETENTION RISK DRIVERS ANALYSIS

Method: Frequency analysis of Q11 (reasons for considering leaving) among faculty who indicated retention risk (Q10 = "Somewhat agree" or "Strongly agree")

Insight for Deans: Identifies specific, actionable factors driving faculty turnover. If workload dominates, focus on workload redistribution; if compensation dominates, focus on salary equity; if climate dominates, focus on leadership development.

Visualization: Horizontal bar chart showing percentage of at-risk faculty citing each reason

Actionable Recommendations:

  • Top 3 drivers: Prioritize interventions addressing these factors
  • If multiple reasons cluster around a theme (e.g., resources = research funding + teaching support), implement comprehensive resource strategy
  • Track over time to assess whether interventions reduce specific drivers

Dashboard Location: Chart 4 - "Retention Risk Drivers"

ANALYSIS 5: WEEKLY WORK HOURS BY DISCIPLINE

Method: Cross-tabulation of Q14 (weekly work hours) by Q5 (discipline), with dual-axis visualization showing both average hours and percentage working 70+ hours

Insight for Deans: Quantifies workload disparities across disciplines. STEM faculty typically work longer hours due to lab obligations. Excessive hours (70+) indicate burnout risk regardless of discipline.

Visualization: Dual-axis chart with bars showing average hours and line showing percentage in extreme workload category

Actionable Recommendations:

  • Disciplines averaging >60 hours: Audit workload expectations, teaching loads, and service requirements
  • High percentage working 70+ hours: Implement workload reduction strategies, hiring additional faculty, or rebalancing responsibilities
  • Correlate with satisfaction and retention risk to identify tipping point where hours become unsustainable

Dashboard Location: Chart 5 - "Weekly Work Hours by Discipline"

ANALYSIS 6: TENURE PROCESS - CLARITY VS. FAIRNESS SCATTER PLOT

Method: Two-dimensional scatter plot mapping Q23 (tenure clarity) against Q24 (tenure fairness) at the college level, with quadrant analysis

Insight for Deans: Reveals which colleges have clear AND fair tenure processes (upper right quadrant = ideal) versus unclear, unfair, or mixed situations. Clarity without fairness suggests transparent but biased processes; fairness without clarity suggests good intentions but poor communication.

Visualization: Scatter plot with four quadrants defined by university mean scores, with college labels

Actionable Recommendations:

  • Lower-left quadrant (low clarity, low fairness): Comprehensive tenure process review required, including criteria documentation, committee training, and transparency improvements
  • Upper-left quadrant (fair but unclear): Focus on communication, mentoring, and documentation of expectations
  • Lower-right quadrant (clear but unfair): Address bias in evaluation through committee diversity and bias training

Dashboard Location: Chart 6 - "Tenure Process: Clarity vs. Fairness"

ANALYSIS 7: INTERSECTIONAL BELONGING ANALYSIS (GENDER × RACE/ETHNICITY)

Method: Cross-tabulation of Q27 (sense of belonging) by Q6 (gender) and Q7 (race/ethnicity) to reveal intersectional experiences, visualized as heatmap

Insight for Deans: Belonging gaps often compound at intersections (e.g., women of color experience lower belonging than white women OR men of color alone). Intersectional analysis reveals which faculty populations face the greatest inclusion challenges.

Visualization: Heatmap with gender on one axis, race/ethnicity on the other, and color intensity representing belonging scores

Actionable Recommendations:

  • Groups scoring <3.0: Require targeted inclusion interventions such as affinity groups, mentoring circles, or climate initiatives
  • Examine whether specific colleges or departments drive disparities through three-way cross-tabulation
  • Create accountability metrics for improving belonging among most disadvantaged groups

Dashboard Location: Chart 7 - "Belonging by Gender × Race/Ethnicity"

ANALYSIS 8: KEY METRICS COMPARISON ACROSS COLLEGES (RADAR CHART)

Method: Multi-dimensional comparison displaying 5-7 key metrics (satisfaction, belonging, psychological safety, workload manageability, compensation satisfaction, etc.) for each college on a radar/spider chart

Insight for Deans: Provides holistic profile of each college's strengths and weaknesses across multiple dimensions. Enables pattern recognition (e.g., College X struggles across all dimensions vs. College Y has specific compensation issues).

Visualization: Radar chart with each axis representing a key metric, overlay of multiple colleges for comparison

Actionable Recommendations:

  • Colleges with consistently low scores across dimensions: Require comprehensive strategic intervention, potentially including leadership changes
  • Colleges with specific weak areas: Target resources to address particular deficits (e.g., if only compensation is low, focus salary equity study)
  • Use as diagnostic tool in annual planning conversations with Deans

Dashboard Location: Chart 8 - "Key Metrics Comparison"

ANALYSIS 9: DEMOGRAPHIC DISTRIBUTION ANALYSIS BY COLLEGE

Method: Descriptive statistics showing distribution of faculty across demographic variables (rank, appointment type, years at institution, discipline, gender, race/ethnicity) within each college

Insight for Deans: Contextualizes satisfaction and experience data. Colleges with very junior faculty may have different needs than those with senior faculty. Demographic composition affects interpretation of survey results.

Visualization: Stacked bar charts or summary tables showing percentage distributions

Actionable Recommendations:

  • Identify colleges with demographic imbalances (e.g., lack of diversity, heavy concentration of adjuncts, aging faculty) requiring recruitment strategy adjustments
  • Understand whether satisfaction differences reflect demographic composition rather than college-specific issues
  • Support workforce planning and succession planning for colleges with high percentages of senior faculty

Dashboard Location: Integrated throughout dashboard as filter options and summary statistics

ANALYSIS 10: QUALITATIVE THEMATIC ANALYSIS OF OPEN-ENDED RESPONSES

Method: Systematic coding and thematic analysis of Q29 (improvement suggestions) and Q30 (additional comments) using qualitative data analysis techniques. Frequency counts of themes, representative quotes, and correlation with quantitative patterns.

Insight for Deans: Open-ended responses provide context, specificity, and unexpected insights not captured by structured items. Reveals "how" and "why" behind quantitative patterns. Faculty voice humanizes data for leadership presentations.

Visualization: Word clouds, frequency tables of themes, and representative quote displays organized by theme

Actionable Recommendations:

  • Most frequently mentioned themes: Prioritize these issues in action planning
  • Surprising or unexpected themes: Flag for further investigation through focus groups or follow-up
  • Use representative quotes in presentations to Deans to illustrate quantitative findings with real faculty voices
  • Cross-reference qualitative themes with quantitative data to validate patterns (e.g., if many faculty mention workload in open-ended responses, confirm with Q12-Q15 quantitative workload items)

Dashboard Location: Thematic summary available as supplementary analysis (qualitative data not visualized in current dashboard prototype)

Human Decision-Making Framework

All substantive intellectual work remained human-directed:

  • Selection of which validated instruments (COACHE, ModernThink, AAU) to draw upon was based on human expertise in institutional research methodologies
  • Determination of which topics to prioritize (satisfaction, retention, workload, belonging, etc.) aligned with the Deans' stated strategic goals
  • Analytical framework design reflecting understanding of higher education research, equity analysis requirements, and actionable insight generation
  • Assessment of which demographic stratification variables enable meaningful equity analysis while protecting respondent confidentiality
  • Judgment regarding survey length, question ordering, and respondent burden considerations
  • Interpretation of how survey results would translate into actionable recommendations for Deans

Rationale for AI Use

AI tools were employed to enhance productivity and technical implementation while maintaining human expertise in institutional research methodology, survey psychometrics, and higher education analytics. This approach mirrors real-world institutional research practice, where AI increasingly serves as a productivity multiplier for technical tasks (data generation, coding, formatting) while domain expertise guides strategic decision-making.

The use of AI for simulated data generation was particularly valuable, enabling creation of a functional interactive dashboard that demonstrates analytical capabilities without access to actual confidential faculty survey data. This approach showcases how survey results would be visualized and interpreted for stakeholder audiences.

Limitations and Considerations

Simulated data, while statistically realistic, does not reflect actual Notre Dame faculty experiences. Real survey implementation would require:

  • IRB review and approval for human subjects research
  • Pilot testing of survey instrument with small faculty sample
  • Validation of psychometric properties (reliability, construct validity)
  • Partnership with faculty governance and Deans to ensure buy-in
  • Confidentiality protocols and secure data storage
  • Professional survey administration platform (Qualtrics, etc.)

The analytical framework and dashboard prototype demonstrate methodological competency but would require adaptation based on actual response patterns, sample sizes within demographic subgroups, and institutional priorities.
